Problem

Haiti faces one of the most extreme cases of poverty seen across the globe. According to the latest household survey (ECVMAS 2012), more than 6 million out of 10.4 million Haitians (59%) were living below the national poverty line of US$2.41 per day and over 2.5 million (24%) were living below the national extreme poverty line of US$1.23 per day. The countries economic issues subject Haitians to the struggles of malnourishment, child labor, and limited education amongst others. Despite the efforts of many organizations, Haiti's economic growth has slowed to 1%.

Cause

Causes of Poverty

There are two major reasons why Haiti is poverty stricken: government instability and susceptibility to natural disasters. A consequence of government instability is the shortage of government funds due to a lack of paid taxes. This leads to poor (or even nonexistent services) such as aid for natural disasters. When these disasters occur, it creates a bigger burden for a country already struggling with few resources. Two natural disasters that have contributed to Haiti's poverty are the 2010 earthquake and 2016's Hurricane Matthew. These events have left thousands of Haitians homeless with many of them still living in relief tents, even today.

Impact

Poverty in Haiti has left many residents fending for their lives. As a result of the poor economy, jobs are scarce and many cannot provide for their families. Children often starve and die off at a young age. According to UNICEF Haiti has the highest rates of infant, under-five and maternal mortality in the Western hemisphere. Poverty has also left many in unstable and insufficient housing conditions.

How to Help

A Red Cross worker helps distribute supplies in Port-au-Prince in 2010

Many foreign and local organizations have contributed aid to Haiti. The most common types are bilateral and multilateral aid; many organizations donate money to help lessen the poverty crisis. There is also a large amount of humanitarian aid, especially after disasters like the earthquake in 2010. Besides these types, there are also some organizations that turn to education or religion as a more sustainable form of aid. For example, AFH, teaches Haitians about religion and healthcare. Other organizations include Hope for Haiti and The Red Cross.
